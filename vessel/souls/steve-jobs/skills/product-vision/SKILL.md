# Product Vision — Vision-Building Skill

The methodology for building product vision from the customer experience backward.
Jobs's core principle: you cannot build a great product by starting with the technology.

**Persona context → read `../../SOUL.md`**
**Knowledge context → read `../../MEMORY.md`**

## The Core Inversion

Most product development runs this sequence:
```
Technology exists → Find use case → Build features → Market to customers
```

Jobs's sequence:
```
Customer experience desired → Define the feeling → Work backward to technology → 
Ship only what serves that experience
```

The inversion changes everything: technology choices, feature decisions, design priorities,
marketing language, pricing strategy. It all flows from the experience you intend to create.

---

## Phase 1: Define the Experience

Before touching a roadmap or spec, answer these three questions:

**Q1: What will someone say to their friend?**
Not: "what are the features?" — What will a real person, after using this product for the
first time, tell someone they care about? This is the product vision in its most honest form.

*Good answer:* "It's like having a thousand songs in your pocket."
*Bad answer:* "It's a high-capacity portable digital audio player with cross-platform sync."

If you can only produce the second answer, you don't have a vision yet. You have a spec.

**Q2: Who is the one person this is for?**
Not a demographic. A specific person, with a specific need, in a specific moment. Jobs's
test: could you describe this person's life before and after? What frustrated them? What
delight do they feel?

**Q3: What does this make possible that wasn't possible before?**
Not: "what does this do better?" — What category of experience now exists that didn't exist?
The iPod didn't make music portable better — there were portable CD players. It made carrying
a library, not a selection. That's a category, not an improvement.

---

## Phase 2: Name What It Actually Is

Write the one-sentence definition. Rules:
- No product category jargon ("a solution for...", "a platform that...")
- No feature listing
- Must produce an emotional response in someone who doesn't know the product
- Must communicate what changes about the person's life, not what the product does

Test it: say it to someone unfamiliar with the product. Do their eyes light up? Do they
immediately ask a follow-up question? If yes, you have it. If they look polite but confused,
you don't.

**The iPhone example:**
Bad: "A touchscreen smartphone with a mobile internet browser and music player."
Better: "The internet in your pocket."
Jobs's: "An iPod. A phone. And an internet communicator... in one device." (The three became
one — and the audience gasped before he said "in one device.")

---

## Phase 3: Work Backwards — The Feature Knife

Now you have the experience. Every feature decision becomes: does this serve the experience
defined in Phase 1?

The Feature Knife question: *If we cut this, does the core experience survive?*
- If yes → strong candidate to cut
- If no → essential

Apply the knife ruthlessly. Not to things that are bad — to things that are good but not
essential. "I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done."

**The list protocol:**
1. List every feature under consideration (10, 30, 100 — list them all)
2. For each: what experience does this serve?
3. Cut everything that serves a secondary experience
4. Of the remaining: rank by how central each is to the *one sentence*
5. Cut the bottom half
6. Now ask: are you sure you can't cut more?

---

## Phase 4: The Technology Question

Only after Phases 1-3: what technology makes this possible?

Constraints are real. But the order matters. When you start with the technology, the product
becomes the technology. When you start with the experience, the technology becomes invisible —
which is exactly right. "Our job is to make things that are so good, the technology
disappears."

Technology questions to ask:
- What's the minimum technology needed to deliver the experience in Phase 1?
- Where does the technology constrain the experience? Can the constraint be removed?
- Where is the technology ahead of the experience? Can the extra capability serve the vision?

---

## Phase 5: The Keynote Test

If you can't present this as a five-minute keynote narrative, you don't understand it yet.

The keynote structure:
1. **The world before** (the pain — stated simply, not dramatically)
2. **What we believed was possible** (the vision — one sentence, said simply)
3. **The thing** (the product — shown, not described)
4. **What this means** (the implication for the customer's life)
5. **One more thing** (the detail that makes it real — the most surprising specific)

The test: run this structure on your product. At each step, ask: is this compelling? If the
audience wouldn't lean forward, it isn't done.
